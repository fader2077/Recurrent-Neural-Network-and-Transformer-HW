Homework 2
Course: RNN and Transformer
Assignment: HW2 – Detect AI Generated Text
Hardware Requirement: NVIDIA RTX 4090 GPU (24GB VRAM)
1. Overview
In this assignment, you will tackle a critical challenge in the era of Generative 
AI: distinguishing between human-written text and text generated by Large 
Language Models (LLMs).
You will utilize the BERT (Bidirectional Encoder Representations from
Transformers) architecture, which we discussed in class, to build a binary 
classifier. Furthermore, you will leverage your high-performance hardware 
(e.g., RTX 4090) to conduct Model Scaling experiments and perform 
Adversarial Attacks using a locally hosted LLM.
Reference Competition: Kaggle - LLM Detect AI Generated Text
2. Dataset Preparation
You may use the official competition dataset or the community-enhanced 
version (recommended for better training stability).
• Source: DAIGT V2 Train Dataset (or similar).
• Labeling:
o 0: Student-written essay (Human).
o 1: LLM-generated essay (AI).
3. Tasks & Requirements
Part 1: Data Analysis & Baseline (20%)
Before diving into Deep Learning, understand your data.
1. EDA (Exploratory Data Analysis): Compare the distribution of word
counts and vocabulary richness between human and AI texts. Are 
there obvious statistical patterns?
2. Classic Baseline: Implement a simple TF-IDF + Logistic Regression
classifier.
3. Report: Record the ROC-AUC score of this baseline. This serves as 
the benchmark your BERT models must beat.
Part 2: BERT Fine-Tuning & Scaling (40%)
You will utilize the Hugging Face transformers library to fine-tune BERT for 
Sequence Classification. You must train two variations to observe the impact 
of model scaling.
1. Model A (Base): Fine-tune bert-base-cased (110M parameters).
2. Model B (Large): Fine-tune bert-large-cased (340M parameters).
o Note: Training bert-large effectively requires significant VRAM. 
Utilize your RTX 4090 to maximize the Batch Size (e.g., 16, 32) 
and utilize fp16 (mixed precision) to speed up training.
3. Metrics: Evaluate both models using ROC-AUC on a held-out 
validation set.
4. Hypothesis: Does the Large model significantly outperform the Base 
model on this specific task? Why or why not?
Part 3: The "Adversarial Attack" with Local LLM (30%)
A static test set is often insufficient. In this part, you will use your GPU to host 
a Generative LLM and attempt to "fool" your own detector.
1. Local LLM Deployment:
o Deploy a modern LLM (e.g., Llama-3-8B-Instruct or Mistral-7B Instruct) locally on your RTX 4090.
o You may use libraries like Ollama (with Python bindings) or 
Hugging Face pipeline.
2. Generation (The Attack):
o Select 5–10 samples of human-written essays from the 
validation set.
o Prompt your local LLM to rewrite these essays to "sound more 
human" or "improve the writing style."
o Example Prompt: "Rewrite the following essay to make it sound 
like it was written by a high school student. [Essay Content]"
3. Testing Defenses:
o Feed these newly generated "Attack Samples" into your best 
BERT classifier from Part 2.
o Analysis: Does the model correctly flag them as AI (Label 1), or 
does it misclassify them as Human (Label 0)? Compare the 
confidence scores (logits/probabilities).
Part 4: Analysis Report (10%)
Submit your source code to Github and write a report (PDF) summarizing your 
findings.
• Performance Comparison: Table showing ROC-AUC for TF-IDF, 
BERT-Base, and BERT-Large.
• Scaling Analysis: Discuss the trade-off between training 
time/resource usage and performance gain when moving from Base to 
Large.
• Adversarial Findings: Show 1-2 examples of text generated by your 
local LLM that successfully fooled (or almost fooled) your detector. 
Discuss why BERT might have failed in these cases.
4. Technical Guidelines
• Tokenizer: Ensure you use the correct tokenizer associated with the 
specific BERT checkpoint (AutoTokenizer.from_pretrained('bert-base cased')).
• Sequence Length: Set max_length to at least 512. If memory allows, 
you can try higher limits, but 512 is standard for BERT.
• Environment: It is recommended to use Docker or a Conda 
environment with torch (with CUDA support) and transformers installed.
5. Submission
• Source Code: .ipynb or .py files (clean and commented).
• Report: A PDF document containing your analysis, charts (Loss 
curves), and example outputs.
